What is Simulation?
• Simulation is the imitation of the operation of a real process or system as a
function of time. Whether done manually or by computer, simulation involves
creating an artificial history of a system and observing that past to conclude
the characteristics of the functions of the real system.
• Simulation methods are used to imitate or mimic real systems, usually via
computer.

--- Page 8 ---
What needs lead us to use it?
• Complexity: many real systems are too complex that their respective models
are virtually impossible to solve mathematically.
• Experimenting with this kind in a real situation is not only costly and time-
consuming but can often prove disastrous. Therefore, every time we must
study problematic situations that we cannot express with mathematical
models that can be solved, we create an experimental model of the real
situation and test it.

Advantages of Simulation
• Flexibility to model things as they are (even if messy and complicated)
• Allows uncertainty, no stationarity in modeling
• Advances in computing/cost ratios
• Advances in simulation software

--- Page 12 ---
Disadvantages
• The construction of systems requires training. It is a skill acquired with time
and experience.
• The results of the simulation may be difficult to interpret. Because simulation
output is basically random variables (usually based on input data that is also
basically random variables), it is often difficult to determine whether an
observation is the result of some relationship of system elements or a result of
randomness.
• Building the simulation model and analyzing the results takes time and
money.

--- Page 13 ---
The Bad news
• Don’t get exact answers, only approximations, estimates
• Also true of many other modern methods
• Can bound errors by machine round off
• Get random output (RIRO) from stochastic simulations
• Statistical design, analysis of simulation experiments
• Exploit: noise control, replicability, sequential sampling, variance-reduction
techniques
• Catch: “standard” statistical methods seldom work

System Definition
• A system is defined as a group-set of objects (elements) that are related to
each other by some constant interaction or interdependence (relationships) to
achieve some goal (goal).
• The basic characteristics of the system as they result from the previous
definition are its elements, the relationships between them and its purpose.
• As an example of a system can be considered an industrial car production
system. Machines, assembly pieces and workers work together along an
assembly line to produce high-quality cars

--- Page 15 ---
The components of a system
To understand and analyze a system it is necessary to determine the meaning of
a series of terms.
• An entity is an object of system interest.
• An attribute is a property of an entity.
• An activity represents an interval of time with a defined duration.
• The state of a system is defined as the group of variables necessary to
describe the system at any time in relation to the objects of study.

--- Page 16 ---
• If a bank is to be studied customers are likely to be one of the entities, the
fluctuation of their transaction volume may be a characteristic and the deposit
may be considered an activity.
• An event is defined as an instantaneous action that can change the state of
the system.

The components of a system
• The term endogenous is used to describe activities and events that take place
within the system while the term exogenous is used to describe activities and
events that take place in the context of the system and that affect the system.
• In the bank study, the arrival of a customer is an exogenous event, and the
completion of a customer's service is an intrinsic event, they change the
state/number of customers in the bank.

Model definition of a system
• Sometimes it is interesting to study a system to understand the relationships
between its elements or to predict how the system will work under new policy.
Studying the system implies that we are likely to experiment with it. But this is
not always possible. A new system may not yet exist. It can be hypothetical
or in the design stage. Even if the system exists, it can be impossible to
experiment with it.

--- Page 19 ---
Model definition of a system
• Consequently, systems studies are often performed through a system model.
• A model is defined as a representation of a system for the purpose of studying
the system. For most studies, it is necessary to consider those aspects/
characteristics of the systems that affect the system under study. These
features are represented in a system model, and the model is a simplification
of the system.
• Model – set of assumptions/approximations about how system works

History of simulation
• Early years (1950s – 1960s)
• Very expensive, specialized tool
• Required big computers, special training
• Mostly in FORTRAN (or even Assembler)
• Formative years (1970s – early 1980s)
• Computers got faster, cheaper
• Value of simulation more widely recognized
• Simulation software improved, but still languages to be learned, typed,
batch processed

--- Page 23 ---
History of simulation
• Recent past (late 1980s – mid 2000s)
• Microcomputer power
• Software expanded into GUIs, animation
• Wider acceptance across more areas
• Traditional manufacturing applications
• Services
• Health care
• “Business processes”
• Still mostly in large firms
• Simulation is often part of “specs”
